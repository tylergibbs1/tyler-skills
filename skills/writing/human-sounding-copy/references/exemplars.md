# Exemplar writers

A living list of writers worth studying, grouped by what they exemplify. Pick the one closest to the genre in play, read a piece of their actual work before drafting, and steal specific moves. For each entry: what to study it for, and what not to borrow.

The point is not to imitate a voice wholesale. It is to have a real human's prose in mind while writing instead of the averaged register a model defaults to. One piece read closely beats ten skimmed.

## Essays and argument

- **Paul Graham** (paulgraham.com/articles.html): the clearest model for plain words carrying hard ideas. Study his sentence rhythm and his habit of stating the counterintuitive claim flatly, without buildup. Don't borrow the occasional confident overreach on subjects outside his expertise.
- **Dan Luu** (danluu.com): argument built from data and personal measurement rather than assertion. Study how he cites specific numbers and names specific systems, and how he engages the strongest opposing view rather than a strawman. The deliberately unpolished formatting is his, not a general model.
- **Ben Thompson**, Stratechery (stratechery.com): the cleanest example of structured argument in tech writing. Study how he sets up a frame, applies it, and names the implication. Don't borrow the recurring proprietary framework vocabulary.
- **Scott Alexander**, Astral Codex Ten (astralcodexten.com): steelmanning and the extended concrete hypothetical. Study how he makes an abstract point by walking through an invented but specific scenario. Way too long for most purposes.
- **George Orwell**, "Politics and the English Language": still the sharpest short piece on how vague writing hides vague thinking. His six rules are worth having memorized, including the sixth, which says break any of them rather than write something barbarous.

## Technical writing and docs

- **Julia Evans** (jvns.ca): the best model for technical writing that admits confusion. Study how she writes "I was confused about X for years" and then explains it, and how short her sentences are. This warmth suits explainers, not reference docs.
- **Simon Willison** (simonwillison.net): plain declarative technical blogging at high volume. Study how he opens by saying exactly what the thing is and why he cared, with no preamble.
- **Rachel Kroll**, rachelbythebay (rachelbythebay.com/w/): terse, voice-heavy war stories from operations work. Study how much personality survives in very short posts, and how the concrete failure carries the lesson without a stated moral.
- **Stripe docs** (stripe.com/docs): the reference standard for docs prose. Study the second person, the imperative voice, and how every concept arrives with a runnable example. Institutional and voiceless by design, which is correct for docs and wrong for essays.
- **Bret Victor** (worrydream.com): essays where the argument and the medium are the same thing. Study the discipline of showing rather than describing.

## Product, marketing, and UI copy

- **Mailchimp Content Style Guide** (mailchimp.com/content-style-guide/): the most useful public guide for voice and tone in product copy, including the writing-for-difficult-moments section. Free and specific.
- **Jason Fried and DHH**, Rework and their posts (world.hey.com/jason): short declarative sentences, strong opinions, no hedging. Study paragraph length and how a whole idea fits in four sentences. The contrarian posture is a house style, not a universal one.
- **David Ogilvy**, Ogilvy on Advertising and his internal memos: specificity as persuasion, and the rule that you cannot bore someone into buying. His memos on how to write are two pages and worth reading whole.
- **Nicole Fenton and Kate Kiefer Lee**, *Nicely Said*: the practical book on writing for the web with a real voice, including voice charts and tone-per-context.
- **Chip and Dan Heath**, *Made to Stick*: why concrete beats abstract, with the mechanism explained. The SUCCESs acronym is a framework, apply it loosely.

## Newsletters and voice-forward writing

- **Matt Levine**, Money Stuff (Bloomberg): probably the single best study in making dry material readable. Long digressive sentences that stay clear, a running set of in-jokes with the reader, jokes that carry actual analysis rather than decorate it. Study how he explains something complicated by talking to you rather than at you. Nearly impossible to imitate directly and embarrassing when done badly.
- **Craig Mod** (craigmod.com): sensory detail and place. Study how much specific physical observation he puts in, and how the essay earns its slower pace.
- **Anne Helen Petersen**, Culture Study (annehelen.substack.com): cultural argument grounded in specific personal and reported detail.
- **Robin Sloan** (robinsloan.com): the newsletter voice, warm and digressive, writing to a person rather than an audience.
- **Molly White** (citationneeded.news): sharp, sourced, unhedged criticism. Study how she makes an accusation and immediately backs it with a citation.
- **John Gruber**, Daring Fireball (daringfireball.net): terse opinion. Study how short a post can be and still land.
- **Venkatesh Rao**, Ribbonfarm (ribbonfarm.com): heavily idiosyncratic idea-writing. Study it as proof that a distinctive voice can carry a strange structure. Do not borrow the density.
- **Maggie Appleton** (maggieappleton.com) and **Nicky Case** (ncase.me): essays where illustration is part of the argument.

## Narrative nonfiction and concreteness

- **Joan Didion**, *The White Album* and *Slouching Towards Bethlehem*: rhythm and the selected detail. Read the opening of "The White Album" out loud.
- **John McPhee**, *Draft No. 4*: structure as a craft problem, from the writer who thinks about it hardest. His essay on the same subject in the New Yorker is the short version.
- **David Foster Wallace**, *A Supposedly Fun Thing I'll Never Do Again*: voice pushed to the limit, obsessive concrete observation. The footnote habit is his and does not transfer.
- **Mary Roach**, *Stiff*: funny nonfiction that stays informative. Study how the joke lands on the specific detail rather than on a quip.
- **Michael Lewis**, *The Big Short* and *Moneyball*: explaining a technical subject through the people who lived it.
- **Oliver Sacks** and **Lewis Thomas**: scientific writing with warmth and first-person curiosity intact.
- **E.B. White**, *Essays of E.B. White*: the plainest good prose in English.

## Books on craft

These are the sources behind `craft-principles.md`. Read the principle there first; go to the book when you want the full treatment.

- **William Zinsser**, *On Writing Well*: clutter, qualifiers, and the significant detail. The one to read first.
- **Joseph Williams**, *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace*: cohesion, old-to-new, the stress position. The most technically useful of these.
- **Steven Pinker**, *The Sense of Style*: the curse of knowledge and classic style.
- **Verlyn Klinkenborg**, *Several Short Sentences About Writing*: sentence-level attention and reading aloud.
- **Roy Peter Clark**, *Writing Tools*: 50 concrete, adoptable techniques.
- **Stephen King**, *On Writing*: adverbs, the second draft, and writing with the door closed.
- **Anne Lamott**, *Bird by Bird*: the permission to write a bad first draft, which is what makes cutting possible.
- **Ursula K. Le Guin**, *Steering the Craft*: rhythm and sentence sound, with exercises.
- **Gerald Graff and Cathy Birkenstein**, *They Say / I Say*: templates for entering an argument and planting a naysayer.
- **Ann Handley**, *Everybody Writes*: the practical one for business and marketing contexts.
- **Stripe Press** (press.stripe.com): consistently well-edited nonfiction; useful as a standard for what edited prose reads like.

## All-lowercase writers

Relevant to this project's house voice (see the house rules in `SKILL.md`). Study how the style reads as deliberate rather than careless.

- **bell hooks**: lowercased her name as a deliberate choice to keep attention on the work rather than the author. Her prose is otherwise formal and rigorous, which is the point: lowercase is not the same as informal.
- **danah boyd** (zephoria.org): lowercase name, academic register. Same lesson.
- The general principle: lowercase works when everything else is disciplined. It reads as careless only when paired with careless writing.

## How to use these

1. Pick the entry closest to the genre and register of what you are writing.
2. Read an actual piece, not a summary of the writer. Fetch it if you have access.
3. Name two or three specific moves before drafting: how they open, how long their sentences run, what they do instead of a conclusion.
4. Draft, then compare against the exemplar rather than against a checklist.

Do not blend five voices at once. That averages back into the generic register this whole skill exists to avoid.

## How to add one

Add a bullet under the right heading with: the writer or work, the one or two things it exemplifies, and the caveat about what not to borrow. Keep entries specific, "study X for Y," not "good writer." Links rot, so a name and title matter more than a URL.
