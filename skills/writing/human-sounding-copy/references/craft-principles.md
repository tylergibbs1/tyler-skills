# The constructive half

Removing tells produces clean text, not human text. What follows is the canonical craft tradition, reduced to things an editor can actually apply. These are the qualities AI writing most conspicuously lacks, and unlike word lists they do not age.

---

## Cohesion: Joseph Williams, *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace*

AI text is usually locally grammatical and globally disconnected. Each sentence is fine; the sequence does not flow. Williams explains why.

**Old to new.** Begin a sentence with information the reader already has, end it with information that is new. The new idea at the end of one sentence becomes the familiar anchor at the start of the next. Chains of these read as thinking; sentences that each start fresh read as a list of facts.

**The stress position.** The most emphatic slot in an English sentence is the end. Put the word you want remembered there, not buried mid-clause. Clark's version is the 2-3-1 rule: strongest at the end, next strongest at the beginning, weakest in the middle.

**Passive voice is sometimes correct.** A blanket "use active voice" rule is wrong. When the passive puts old information first and new information last, it serves cohesion, and it wins. Choose voice by what it does to the flow, not by rule.

**Subject and verb early.** Start most sentences with the subject and verb rather than a stack of introductory clauses. Clark: "begin sentences with subjects and verbs."

**Applying it:** read consecutive sentence openings in a paragraph. If they do not pick up anything from the previous sentence's ending, the paragraph has no cohesion regardless of how clean the sentences are.

## Reader modeling: Steven Pinker, *The Sense of Style*

**The curse of knowledge** is Pinker's "single best explanation I know of why good people write bad prose": once you know something, you cannot reconstruct what it is like not to know it, so you skip the step the reader needed. Models have a version of this too, having absorbed everything and modeling no one.

**Classic style** treats writing as directing the reader's gaze at something in the world. The writer is not performing thought, not covering themselves, not summarizing a field. They are pointing at a thing and saying look at that.

**Measured hedging.** Pinker defends dropping hedges: you can count on the common sense of readers to fill in the qualifications. This nuances any hard anti-hedging rule. One real hedge on a genuinely uncertain claim is good writing. Three stacked hedges on a safe claim is cowardice.

**Applying it:** name explicitly what the reader does not yet know, and delete the explanation of what they do.

## Concreteness: William Zinsser, *On Writing Well*

**The significant detail** beats the characterization. "Don't tell us that the radio blared loudly." Give the specific thing and let the reader draw the conclusion. Emotion is shown through concrete detail: "my hands shook," not "I was nervous."

**Prune the qualifiers.** "A bit," "a little," "sort of," "very," "quite." Zinsser: every little qualifier whittles away some fraction of the reader's trust.

**Ethos.** "Readers want a writer who believes in himself and in what he is saying." A writer with something to lose, a real opinion, a real risk, accountability for a claim, reads differently from committee-safe prose. This is the quality most absent from AI text and the hardest to fake.

**Endings.** Zinsser prefers a surprising ending, often a quotation, to a summarizing one. Never land a piece by recapping it.

**Applying it:** every paragraph that makes a claim gets a named entity, a number, a date, or a specific situation. No exceptions in a piece of any length.

## Rhythm: Gary Provost, Roy Peter Clark, Verlyn Klinkenborg

Provost's demonstration is the whole lesson: a run of five-word sentences is monotonous no matter how correct each one is, and varying the length creates music. This is burstiness, arrived at by ear rather than by statistic.

**Clark's applicable tools** (*Writing Tools*): order words for emphasis; establish a pattern, then break it; let punctuation control pace and space; begin sentences with subjects and verbs.

**Klinkenborg** (*Several Short Sentences About Writing*): good writing is one strong sentence after another. The ear catches what the eye misses, so read aloud, always. And: "everything you notice is important." What a writer chooses to notice is a large part of what makes them recognizable.

**Compression.** Klinkenborg again: write for a reader you can trust and the need to overexplain disappears. What is left out, the implication, the ellipsis, is a human signal. Models never leave anything out.

**Applying it:** read the draft aloud. Any sentence you stumble on gets rewritten, not defended.

---

## Argument: Gerald Graff and Cathy Birkenstein, *They Say / I Say*

**Plant a naysayer.** Human persuasive writing anticipates the strongest objection and answers it. LLM text asserts into a vacuum, or performs balance without engaging either side. Name the objection in the objector's own terms, then answer it.

**Answer "so what? who cares?"** State why this matters, to whom, and what changes. This is the real version of the significance that inflated language fakes. Deleting "underscores the pivotal importance of" leaves a hole; fill it with the actual consequence rather than leaving the claim floating.

**Enter a conversation.** Position the claim against what someone else said. Human writing is nearly always responding to something.

---

## Productive imperfection

A controlled amount of asymmetry reads as human: self-correction mid-thought, a deliberate fragment, a digression that pays off, structure that is not forced into parallel.

**The hard limit:** never add typos, grammatical errors, or random noise. That is what bad humanizer tools do, it degrades quality, and it does not fool anything. Imperfection has to be the kind a good writer chooses, not the kind a careless one leaves.

---

## The failure mode of this file

Applying all of it mechanically produces its own uniform register: the de-AI'd voice, all short punchy sentences and mandatory contractions and one concrete detail per paragraph like a quota. Use these to diagnose what a specific piece is missing, not as a checklist to satisfy on every piece.
